# Prompt Examples

## Readability Upgrade Pattern (General Users)

Use this pattern when the user asks a practical tax question and does not request a legal memo.

Old pattern to avoid:

- long legal explanation first
- practical recommendation buried at the end

Required upgrade:

1. `Bottom Line` (1-2 sentences)
2. `Quick Read (3 Key Points)` (exactly 3 bullets)
3. `What To Do Now` (3-5 action bullets)
4. then legal detail (`Why`, `Risk`, `Authorities`, `Sources`)

This makes the difference visible even to non-lawyers.

Default compact target:

- 180-260 words before `Sources`
- hard cap about 300 words before `Sources`
- 3-4 source links max

## Compact Route Templates (All Question Types)

Use one route only.

### Practical yes/no

- `Bottom Line`
- `Quick Read (3 Key Points)`
- `What To Do Now`
- `Risk`
- `Sources`

### Compare / rank options

- `Bottom Line`
- `Top Differences` (3-5 bullets)
- `Recommendation`
- `Sources`

### Filing/process question

- `Bottom Line`
- `Steps`
- `Forms and Deadlines`
- `Risk`
- `Sources`

### Authority reliability check

- `Bottom Line`
- `Holding and Limits`
- `Use / Do Not Use`
- `Sources`

## California Nonresident Sole Proprietor Sourcing

Question:

- "After moving from California to Nevada, if I am a sole proprietor who performs 100% of services from Nevada for California clients, is that income still California-source taxable?"

Expected opening:

> Possibly. FTB may assert California-source income if California clients receive the benefit of your services in California, but the answer depends on current statutes, regulations, published case law, FTB guidance, and the precise facts.

Expected behavior:

- Start with the direct answer.
- Classify the taxpayer as a sole proprietor, not a W-2 employee.
- Separate residency from source income.
- Explain FTB’s practical customer-benefit position.
- Check current directly relevant published cases before finalizing.
- Run a date-stamped recent case-law sweep for this time-sensitive wording (`still California-source taxable`).
- Sweep at least: California published/citable appellate opinions, OTA precedential opinions, and current FTB legal-ruling/notice/tax-news updates.
- If FTB guidance or OTA authority relies on a legal theory, check whether a published court decision has accepted, rejected, limited, or questioned that theory.
- If an OTA precedential opinion conflicts with a later published Court of Appeal decision, explain that the court decision carries greater weight.
- Do not treat FTB webpages as binding law.
- Do not give a categorical answer if authority is mixed.
- State what facts could change the answer.
- In `Sources`, include `freshness check date` and a short `query -> source checked -> result` log.
- For subsection-level claims, verify the subsection text and taxpayer-role fit before citing it.
- If the recent-case sweep is incomplete, keep the conclusion preliminary (`Possibly`/`It depends`) rather than high-confidence.
- Make the first screen readable for non-lawyers: short paragraph + 3-key-point summary + action checklist.

Expected structure:

1. Bottom Line
2. Quick Read (3 Key Points)
3. What This Means for You
4. What To Do Now
5. Why
6. Risk
7. Authorities (Most Important Only)
8. Sources

Bad answer patterns:

- "You work in Nevada, so it is not California-source."
- "California clients automatically make it California-source."
- "FTB guidance is binding law."
- "A single case resolves all similar taxpayers."
- "RTC § 25136 alone directly taxes nonresident sole proprietors."
- "A future appellate decision could change this" without checking whether one already exists.
- "No new cases" without showing search date and checked sources.
- "No appellate cases found" after running only 2022-2025 queries and skipping current-year California Courts published/citable search.
- "No appellate cases found" without running an agency-name query (`Franchise Tax Board` + current year) on California Courts published/citable opinions.
- Sweep freshness date says 2026 but queries only cover up to 2025.
- Citing `CCR 17951-4(d)(1)` as a sole-proprietor customer-benefit rule without verifying subsection text and taxpayer-role fit.
- Giving a high-confidence bottom line after admitting the recent-case sweep was not completed.
- Treating an OTA precedential opinion as stronger than a later published Court of Appeal decision.
- Large dense legal paragraphs before giving a clear practical recommendation.
- Repeating the same disclaimer multiple times.
- 600+ word answers for a practical yes/no question.
- Saying a case `directly overruled` prior administrative precedent without explicit language in the opinion.
- Starting with process narration (`Now I'll run the sweep...`, `I have enough to answer`).
- Using hype wording (`landmark`, `dramatic`) instead of precise legal effect language.
- Using only commentary links for key cases while omitting official court/OTA links.

Better answer pattern:

> Possibly. For sole proprietors, FTB practical guidance may focus on where customers receive the benefit of the service, but that guidance must be weighed against applicable statutes, regulations, and current published case law. The answer is strongest when framed as risk-based rather than categorical.

Suggested `Quick Read (3 Key Points)` example:

> - FTB may still treat this as California-source income because your clients receive the benefit in California.  
> - You still have a taxpayer-side argument because all services were physically performed in Nevada.  
> - Your residency documentation and the latest appellate/OTA authority will most affect the filing outcome.

Suggested `What To Do Now` example:

> - Save proof of Nevada domicile change (license, voter registration, lease, banking).  
> - Keep work logs proving services were performed outside California.  
> - Review filing posture (report vs protective position) with a California CPA/tax attorney before filing.

Suggested compact answer skeleton:

> Bottom Line: [1-2 sentences]  
> Quick Read: [3 bullets]  
> What To Do Now: [3-5 bullets]  
> Risk: [2-4 bullets + one swing-fact line]  
> Sources: [3-4 links]

Suggested source-log add-on for this question:

> Freshness check date: YYYY-MM-DD  
> Recent case-law sweep log:  
> - Query: `California nonresident sole proprietor service income source` -> Source: California appellate published opinions -> Result: [short result]  
> - Query: `site:ota.ca.gov nonresident sourcing precedential` -> Source: OTA precedential opinions -> Result: [short result]  
> - Query: `CCR 17951-4 nonresident sole proprietor` -> Source: FTB legal-ruling/notice/tax-news pages -> Result: [short result]

## California W-2 Remote Worker Sourcing

Question:

- "I moved from California to Texas but still work remotely as a W-2 employee for a California employer. Are my post-move wages California-source?"

Expected opening:

> Probably not, if you are a bona fide nonresident and performed all post-move services outside California. But special rules may apply to deferred compensation, equity compensation, bonuses, or part-year residency.

Expected behavior:

- Start with the direct answer.
- Classify the taxpayer as a W-2 employee.
- Apply the physical-work-location wage rule.
- Separate residency from wage sourcing.
- Mention special compensation items.
- Do not apply sole proprietor customer-benefit rules to W-2 wages.
- Check current FTB guidance and any directly relevant authority before finalizing.

Bad answer patterns:

- "California employer means California-source wages."
- "Moving out eliminates all California filing duties."
- "Customer-benefit sourcing applies to W-2 wages."
- Ignoring part-year residency.
- Ignoring deferred or equity compensation.

Better answer pattern:

> Probably not for ordinary post-move W-2 wages, assuming you truly became a nonresident and performed no services in California after the move. But California may still tax resident-period wages, California workdays, and certain deferred or equity-based compensation tied to prior California services.

## California Residency vs Source Income

Question:

- "I moved out of California. Does that mean California can no longer tax me?"

Expected opening:

> Not necessarily. Moving out may end California taxation of worldwide income after you become a nonresident, but California can still tax California-source income.

Expected behavior:

- Start with the direct answer.
- Separate domicile/residency from source income.
- Identify move-year part-year treatment.
- Explain that nonresidents may still file if they have California-source income.
- State assumptions about days, home, family, business ties, and income type.
- Do not make the sourcing answer depend only on residency.

Bad answer patterns:

- "You moved out, so California cannot tax you."
- "Any California connection means California taxes everything."
- Combining residency and sourcing into one conclusion.
- Ignoring move-year part-year residency.

Better answer pattern:

> Moving out matters, but it does not end the analysis. First determine whether the taxpayer actually became a nonresident. Then determine whether any post-move income remains California-source.

## Federal + California Conformity

Question:

- "Summarize federal bonus depreciation and California decoupling for the 2025 tax year. Add authority ranks."

Expected opening:

> It depends on the tax year and property type, but California often does not automatically conform to federal bonus depreciation.

Expected behavior:

- Start with the direct answer.
- Start authority review with IRC and Treasury Regulations.
- Then check California conformity statutes.
- Then use FTB guidance for filing mechanics.
- Separate law from forms, webpages, and commentary.
- Verify the tax year.
- Provide source links.

Bad answer patterns:

- "California follows federal depreciation."
- Giving commentary-only support.
- Ignoring the tax year.
- Treating FTB webpages as binding law.

Better answer pattern:

> Federal law may allow the deduction, but California conformity must be checked separately. California often decouples from federal depreciation rules, so the answer depends on the California conformity statute and tax year.

## FTB Notice Response

Question:

- "Draft an FTB notice response and label nonprecedential material."

Expected opening:

> I can draft it, but the response should separate controlling authority from practical guidance and research leads.

Expected behavior:

- Start with the response position.
- Identify notice type, tax year, taxpayer type, and issue.
- Use RTC, CCR, formal FTB authority, and precedential opinions first.
- Label FTB webpages and publications as practical guidance.
- Label nonprecedential OTA or Chief Counsel material clearly.
- State missing facts or authority gaps.
- Provide a draft only after the authority position is clear.

Bad answer patterns:

- Relying on nonprecedential opinions as controlling.
- Using aggressive advocacy without authority support.
- Omitting the tax year or notice issue.
- Treating FTB webpages as law.

Better answer pattern:

> The response should rely first on statutes, regulations, and formal authority. Practical guidance can support filing mechanics or agency expectations, but it should not be presented as controlling law.

## CDTFA Sales / Use Tax

Question:

- "I found one CDTFA annotation. Is that enough for a filing position?"

Expected opening:

> Probably not by itself. A CDTFA annotation is usually a research lead, not controlling authority.

Expected behavior:

- Start with the direct answer.
- Check RTC sales/use provisions.
- Check CDTFA regulations.
- Look for precedential OTA/BOE authority.
- Use annotations cautiously.
- State if the position is not locked.
- Explain whether the annotation matches the facts.

Bad answer patterns:

- Treating annotations as binding.
- Ignoring statutes and regulations.
- Giving a confident conclusion from one annotation.
- Omitting factual differences.

Better answer pattern:

> The annotation may help identify the issue, but it is not enough by itself. Look for supporting statute, regulation, or precedential authority before relying on the position.

## EDD Payroll Classification

Question:

- "Analyze this EDD worker classification issue from a payroll tax perspective."

Expected opening:

> This requires California payroll tax analysis, and worker classification may also require employment-law review.

Expected behavior:

- Start with the direct answer.
- Identify the payroll tax issue.
- Start with CUIC and regulations.
- Use California cases where applicable.
- Then use EDD guidance and forms.
- Separate payroll tax from employment-law conclusions.
- Include the required employment-law caution.

Bad answer patterns:

- Treating EDD webpages as controlling law.
- Ignoring CUIC.
- Giving broad employment-law advice when asked for payroll tax.
- Assuming federal classification rules fully control California payroll tax.

Better answer pattern:

> For payroll tax, start with California payroll authority. EDD guidance matters, but it should be separated from binding law. Worker classification may also require employment-law review.

## Case-Law Overread Check

Question:

- "Can I cite this case to support my California sourcing position?"

Expected opening:

> Maybe, but first read the case narrowly and confirm its current status.

Expected behavior:

- Identify court, year, publication status, and current status.
- State taxpayer type and issue decided.
- State procedural posture.
- State the holding narrowly.
- State what the case did not decide.
- Compare to the user’s facts.
- Check whether later authority limited it.
- Do not use the case beyond its holding.

Bad answer patterns:

- Treating dicta as holding.
- Using a pass-through case for a sole proprietor rule.
- Using an employee case for a contractor rule.
- Ignoring later authority or review status.
- Treating a summary judgment ruling as resolving every legal theory unless it actually does.

Better answer pattern:

> The case may help, but only for the proposition it actually decided. Identify the taxpayer type, theory, procedural posture, holding, and unresolved issues before relying on it.

## Local Business Tax

Question:

- "Does San Francisco business tax apply if my company has customers there but no office there?"

Expected opening:

> It depends on the specific local tax, tax year, and municipal code. Customer location may matter, but the analysis must start with local law.

Expected behavior:

- Start with the direct answer.
- Identify city/county.
- Identify tax type.
- Start with municipal code.
- Check local regulations and official guidance.
- Do not import FTB sourcing rules without checking the ordinance.
- Track effective date and tax year.

Bad answer patterns:

- Assuming no office means no tax.
- Assuming customers alone always create tax.
- Applying state income tax sourcing without local authority.
- Ignoring tax year.

Better answer pattern:

> Local tax turns on the local ordinance. Customer location may matter, but the municipal code and city guidance control the analysis.

## Entity Compliance

Question:

- "My California LLC is suspended. What controls reinstatement?"

Expected opening:

> Reinstatement usually requires addressing both SOS entity status and FTB tax compliance.

Expected behavior:

- Start with the direct answer.
- Identify entity type.
- Separate SOS status from FTB tax suspension.
- Use Corporations Code, SOS materials, and FTB revivor guidance.
- Give compliance steps after authority.

Bad answer patterns:

- Treating SOS status as the whole issue.
- Ignoring FTB suspension.
- Giving generic steps without entity type.
- Mixing entity status and tax compliance.

Better answer pattern:

> Entity reinstatement usually has both SOS and FTB components. Resolve entity status and tax compliance separately.

## Weak Authority

Question:

- "I found a blog post and one nonprecedential OTA opinion. Can I rely on this?"

Expected opening:

> Not safely by itself. That is research support, not a locked filing position.

Expected behavior:

- Start with the direct answer.
- Label commentary as commentary.
- Label nonprecedential material as nonprecedential.
- Search for stronger authority.
- State if support is insufficient.

Required language:

> Current materials are insufficient to lock a filing position. The items below are research leads or practical guidance only.

Bad answer patterns:

- Treating commentary as authority.
- Treating nonprecedential OTA opinions as controlling.
- Saying “should be fine” without primary support.
- Hiding weak support behind confident wording.

Better answer pattern:

> This is a research lead, not a filing-position conclusion. Look for statute, regulation, precedential authority, or formal agency guidance before relying on it.

## Formal Research Memo

Question:

- "Prepare a formal California tax research memo."

Expected opening:

> Bottom Line: [Answer directly in 1-3 sentences.]

Expected structure:

1. Bottom Line
2. Issue
3. Facts / Assumptions
4. Authorities
5. Analysis
6. Filing Guidance
7. Risk
8. Sources

Expected behavior:

- Include assumptions.
- Separate binding authority from practical guidance.
- Label nonprecedential material.
- Discuss conflicting authority.
- Avoid hiding uncertainty in formal language.

Bad answer patterns:

- Starting with `Issue` and delaying the answer.
- Omitting assumptions.
- Treating guidance as law.
- Giving a filing-position conclusion without strong support.

Better answer pattern:

> Bottom Line first, then formal analysis. A good memo is clear about authority rank, assumptions, conflicts, and unresolved issues.
