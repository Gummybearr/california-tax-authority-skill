# Prompt Examples

## Federal + California Conformity

Question:

- "Summarize federal bonus depreciation and California decoupling for the 2025 tax year. Add authority ranks."

Expected opening:

> Bottom Line: California generally does not automatically conform to federal bonus depreciation. The answer depends on the tax year, property type, and the specific California conformity statute or adjustment rule.

Expected behavior:

- Start with a direct bottom-line answer before the authority stack.
- Start with IRC and Treasury Regulations.
- Then analyze California conformity through RTC, CCR, and FTB guidance.
- Separate legal authority from forms, instructions, publications, webpages, and commentary.
- Include official or otherwise verifiable links for each core citation.
- Do not use commentary to establish the filing position.
- If California conformity is partial, delayed, or decoupled, state that clearly.
- If tax-year applicability is uncertain, say so and request or identify the relevant tax year.

Expected structure:

1. Bottom Line
2. Why
3. Authorities
4. Application
5. Filing Guidance
6. Risk
7. Sources

Bad answer pattern to avoid:

- Starting with a long authority stack before answering the direct question.
- Treating FTB publications or webpages as binding law.
- Assuming California conforms to a federal rule without checking California conformity.
- Giving a filing-position answer based only on commentary.

## California Nonresident Sole Proprietor Sourcing

Question:

- "After moving from California to Nevada, if I am a sole proprietor who performs 100% of services from Nevada for California clients, is that income still California-source taxable?"

Expected opening:

> Possibly yes. Even if you perform 100% of the services from Nevada, California may still treat the income as California-source if your California clients receive the benefit of the services in California. The key distinction is that you are a sole proprietor, not a W-2 employee.

Expected behavior:

- Start with the direct answer first.
- Do not answer solely by saying “services are performed in Nevada, so the income is not California-source.”
- First classify the taxpayer as a sole proprietor / Schedule C business, not a W-2 employee.
- Explain that current FTB practical guidance for independent contractors and sole proprietors looks to where the customer receives the benefit of the service.
- State that if California customers receive the benefit of the services in California, California may treat the related income as California-source.
- Separately discuss:
  - nonresident vs part-year resident status
  - California workdays
  - California business presence
  - Schedule R / apportionment
  - Form 540NR filing obligation
  - customer-by-customer or receipts-based sourcing
- Label FTB webpages and instructions as practical guidance or administrative guidance, not binding law.
- Include source links.

Expected structure:

1. Bottom Line
2. Why
3. Authorities
4. Application
5. Filing Guidance
6. Risk
7. Sources

Bad answer pattern to avoid:

- "Because all services are performed in Nevada, the income is generally not California-source even if all clients are in California."
- Applying the W-2 employee physical-work-location rule to a sole proprietor.
- Treating the client’s California location as irrelevant.
- Treating FTB webpages as binding law.
- Saying RTC § 25136 alone directly taxes the nonresident sole proprietor.
- Saying CCR § 17951-4 by itself creates a simple customer-location rule without explaining the apportionment framework.
- Citing Valentino as controlling authority for a sole proprietor remote-service fact pattern.
- Starting with a long authority stack before answering the user’s direct question.

Better answer pattern:

> Possibly yes. For an independent contractor or sole proprietor, current FTB practical guidance focuses on where the customer receives the benefit of the service, not merely where the contractor physically performs the work. If California clients receive the benefit in California, California may treat the related income as California-source. This is separate from whether California can tax worldwide income, which depends on residency.

Authority precision notes:

- RTC § 17041 and RTC § 17951 provide the general statutory basis for taxing nonresidents on California-source income.
- CCR Title 18 § 17951-4 connects nonresident business income to California allocation/apportionment rules when a trade or business is carried on within and outside California.
- RTC § 25136 provides the market-assignment rule for service receipts under California apportionment.
- FTB scenario-based guidance is practical guidance showing FTB’s current administrative position for independent contractors and sole proprietors.
- Do not label FTB webpages as binding law.
- Do not overstate a regulation, statute, or case beyond what it directly supports.

## California W-2 Remote Worker Sourcing

Question:

- "I moved from California to Texas but still work remotely as a W-2 employee for a California employer. I never returned to California after the move. Are my post-move wages California-source?"

Expected opening:

> Probably not, if you are a bona fide nonresident and performed all post-move W-2 services outside California. For W-2 wages, California generally looks to where the employee physically performed the services, subject to special rules such as deferred compensation or equity-based compensation.

Expected behavior:

- Start with the direct answer first.
- Classify the taxpayer as a W-2 employee.
- Apply the physical-work-location rule for nonresident wages.
- Explain that post-move wages are generally not California-source if all services are performed outside California.
- Add caveats for:
  - deferred compensation
  - equity-based compensation
  - bonuses tied to prior California work
  - part-year residency
  - weak domicile facts
  - California workdays after the move
- Do not apply the sole proprietor customer-benefit rule to W-2 wages.
- Separately discuss residency and sourcing.

Expected structure:

1. Bottom Line
2. Why
3. Authorities
4. Application
5. Filing Guidance
6. Risk
7. Sources

Bad answer pattern to avoid:

- Applying the independent contractor / sole proprietor customer-benefit rule to W-2 wages.
- Ignoring part-year residency.
- Ignoring deferred compensation or equity compensation.
- Assuming that a California employer automatically makes wages California-source.
- Assuming that moving out of California automatically eliminates all California filing obligations.

Better answer pattern:

> Probably not for ordinary post-move W-2 wages, assuming you truly became a nonresident and performed no services in California after the move. But California may still tax wages earned during the California resident period, wages tied to California workdays, and certain deferred or equity-based compensation connected to prior California services.

## California Residency vs Source-Income Distinction

Question:

- "I moved out of California. Does that mean California can no longer tax my income?"

Expected opening:

> Not necessarily. Moving out of California may stop California from taxing your worldwide income after you become a nonresident, but California can still tax California-source income earned during the nonresident period.

Expected behavior:

- Start with the direct answer first.
- Separate:
  - residency/domicile
  - source income
  - filing obligation
  - apportionment/allocation
- Explain that California residents are generally taxed on worldwide income.
- Explain that nonresidents are generally taxed only on California-source income.
- Explain that part-year residents may have both resident-period and nonresident-period income reporting.
- Identify facts needed to determine residency:
  - domicile
  - closest connections
  - days in California
  - home ownership or lease
  - family location
  - driver’s license
  - voter registration
  - business ties
  - professional and financial ties
- Avoid treating residency as dispositive of source-income issues.

Expected structure:

1. Bottom Line
2. Why
3. Authorities
4. Application
5. Filing Guidance
6. Risk
7. Sources

Bad answer pattern to avoid:

- "You moved out, so California cannot tax you anymore."
- "You have California clients, so California taxes everything."
- Combining residency and source-income analysis into one undifferentiated conclusion.
- Ignoring part-year resident treatment in the move year.

Better answer pattern:

> Moving out of California helps only if the residency change is genuine. After that, the question becomes whether you still have California-source income. Those are separate issues.

## FTB Notice Response

Question:

- "Draft an FTB notice response. Label any nonprecedential material."

Expected opening:

> Bottom Line: I can draft the response, but I will separate controlling authority from practical guidance and nonprecedential research leads so the filing position is not overstated.

Expected behavior:

- Start with a direct summary of the response position.
- Identify the notice type, tax year, taxpayer type, and disputed issue.
- Start authority review with RTC, CCR, FTB Legal Rulings, FTB Notices, and precedential OTA opinions where available.
- Use FTB publications, forms, and webpages as practical guidance.
- Label FTB Chief Counsel Rulings and OTA nonprecedential opinions as nonprecedential or research leads.
- Do not use nonprecedential material as controlling authority.
- Include an `External Sources (Links)` or `Sources` section mapping each cited item to a URL.
- If authority support is weak, explicitly state that the position needs more support before filing.
- Keep the draft response separate from the authority analysis if the user asks for both.

Expected structure:

1. Bottom Line
2. Draft Response Summary
3. Authorities
4. Application to Notice
5. Nonprecedential / Research Leads
6. Risk and Missing Facts
7. Sources
8. Draft Letter, if requested

Bad answer pattern to avoid:

- Citing nonprecedential OTA opinions as controlling.
- Citing FTB webpages as binding law.
- Drafting an aggressive filing-position response without enough primary or strong authority.
- Omitting the tax year or notice issue.
- Mixing legal authority and advocacy language without labels.

Better answer pattern:

> The response should rely first on applicable RTC and CCR provisions. Any FTB publication or webpage can support filing mechanics or show the agency’s practical position, but it should not be described as controlling law. Any OTA nonprecedential opinion should be labeled as fact-pattern support only.

- Search for applicable RTC provisions, CDTFA regulations, and precedential OTA/BOE authority.
- If statute/regulation/case support is missing, state that the authority is insufficient to lock the filing position.
- Include the risk limitation clearly.
- Do not present CDTFA annotations as controlling law.
- Explain whether the annotation is factually aligned or distinguishable.
- Include official CDTFA or legal source links if available.

Expected structure:

1. Bottom Line
2. Why
3. Authorities
4. Application
5. Filing Guidance
6. Risk
7. Sources

- Treating a CDTFA annotation as binding authority.

Better answer pattern:

> The annotation is useful, but it is not enough by itself. I would treat it as a research lead and look for supporting RTC provisions, CDTFA regulations, and precedential OTA or BOE authority before relying on the position.

## Case-Law Overread Check

Question:

- "Can I cite Valentino to say California cannot tax a nonresident consultant who performs all services outside California?"

Expected opening:

> Be careful. Valentino should not be cited as broad controlling authority for the rule that a nonresident sole proprietor’s service income is sourced only where the services are physically performed.

Expected behavior:

- Start with the direct caution first.
- Do not accept the citation at face value.
- Explain what Valentino actually involved.
- Determine whether the taxpayer was:
  - a pass-through owner
  - sole proprietor
  - W-2 employee
  - independent contractor
  - corporate taxpayer
  - other
- If the taxpayer is a sole proprietor or independent contractor, prioritize the current FTB customer-benefit guidance.
- Label Valentino only for the proposition it actually supports.
- Do not use Valentino to override a directly applicable FTB sole proprietor / independent contractor scenario.
- If a case is factually mismatched, say so explicitly.

Expected structure:

1. Bottom Line
2. What the Case Actually Involved
3. Why It May Not Control
4. Better Authorities to Check
5. Application
6. Risk
7. Sources

Bad answer pattern to avoid:

- "Valentino says services are sourced where performed."
- Using Valentino as the primary authority for a Schedule C sole proprietor.
- Ignoring the taxpayer structure in Valentino.
- Ignoring current FTB practical guidance.
- Treating a case as broader than its facts and holding.

Better answer pattern:

> Valentino may be relevant background for pass-through entity sourcing, but it should not be treated as the controlling rule for a nonresident sole proprietor performing remote services. For a sole proprietor or independent contractor, check current FTB practical guidance and the apportionment framework before relying on Valentino.

## Local Business Tax

Question:

- "Does San Francisco business tax apply if my company has customers there but no office there?"

Expected opening:

> It depends on the specific local tax, the tax year, and the city’s nexus and apportionment rules. Customer location may matter for local gross receipts taxes, but the answer must start with the municipal code and official city guidance.

Expected behavior:

- Start with the direct answer first.
- Identify the city or county.
- Identify the tax type:
  - gross receipts tax
  - business license tax
  - payroll expense tax
  - commercial rents tax
  - other local tax
- Start with municipal code.
- Then check local regulations, official city/county guidance, forms, and FAQs.
- Track:
  - city/county name
  - tax year
  - effective date
  - last updated date
- Do not apply state income tax sourcing rules automatically to local business taxes.
- Include official city/county source links.

Expected structure:

1. Bottom Line
2. Why
3. Authorities
4. Application
5. Filing Guidance
6. Risk
7. Sources

Bad answer pattern to avoid:

- Applying FTB sourcing rules to a local tax without checking the municipal code.
- Ignoring tax year and local law changes.
- Treating local FAQs as binding law.
- Assuming no office means no local tax exposure.
- Assuming customers alone always create local tax exposure.

Better answer pattern:

> The answer depends on the municipal code and the specific local tax. For local gross receipts taxes, customer location may be relevant, but the analysis must be based on the city’s ordinance and official guidance, not just general California income tax sourcing rules.

  - minimum tax or fees

  - revivor process

## Weak Authority / Research Lead Only

Question:

- "I found a blog post and one nonprecedential OTA opinion. Can I rely on this position?"

Expected opening:

> Not safely by itself. A blog post and a nonprecedential OTA opinion are research leads, not enough to lock a filing position without stronger authority.

Expected behavior:

- Start with the direct answer first.
- Label the blog post as commentary.
- Label the OTA opinion as nonprecedential unless it is precedential.
- Search for stronger authority:
  - statute
  - regulation
  - binding/citable precedent
  - precedential OTA/BOE opinion
  - FTB Legal Ruling or Notice
- If no stronger authority is found, explicitly say the position is not locked.
- Do not overstate confidence.
- Include the required limitation.

Expected structure:

1. Bottom Line
2. Why
3. Available Materials
4. Missing Authorities
5. Risk
6. Sources

Required language:

> Current materials are insufficient to lock a filing position. The items below are research leads or practical guidance only.

Bad answer pattern to avoid:

- Treating commentary as legal authority.
- Treating nonprecedential OTA opinions as controlling.
- Saying the taxpayer “should be fine” without primary or strong authority.
- Omitting the confidence limitation.

Better answer pattern:

> This is a research lead, not a filing-position conclusion. I would not rely on it without finding supporting statute, regulation, precedential authority, or formal agency guidance.

Question:

- "Prepare a formal California tax research memo on this sourcing issue."

Expected opening:

> Bottom Line: [Give the direct answer in 1-3 sentences before the formal memo sections.]

Expected behavior:

- Even in formal memo mode, include a short bottom line first.
- Then use the full authority-first memo format.
- Include:
  - Issue
  - Facts / Assumptions
  - Controlling Authorities
  - Administrative / Practical Guidance
  - Analysis
  - Practical Filing Guidance
  - Nonprecedential / Research Leads
  - Risk and Review
  - External Sources (Links)
- Clearly separate assumptions from conclusions.
- If facts are missing, state assumptions and explain how the conclusion could change.
- Do not hide uncertainty inside legal-sounding language.
- Include official or otherwise verifiable links.

Expected structure:

1. Bottom Line
2. Issue
3. Facts / Assumptions
4. Controlling Authorities
5. Administrative / Practical Guidance
6. Analysis
7. Practical Filing Guidance
8. Nonprecedential / Research Leads
9. Risk and Review
10. External Sources (Links)

Bad answer pattern to avoid:

- Starting with `Issue` and delaying the answer until the end.
- Omitting assumptions.
- Treating practical guidance as controlling authority.
- Failing to label nonprecedential sources.
- Giving a filing-position conclusion without strong authority.

Better answer pattern:

> Bottom Line: Possibly yes. California may treat the income as California-source if the California customer receives the benefit of the service in California. The formal memo below separates the binding statutory/regulatory framework from FTB practical guidance and nonprecedential research leads.
